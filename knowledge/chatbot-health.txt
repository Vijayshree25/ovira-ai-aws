Women's Menstrual & Reproductive Health Reference

SECTION 1: MENSTRUAL CYCLE BASICS
-----------------------------------
The menstrual cycle is the monthly series of changes a woman's body goes through in preparation for the possibility of pregnancy. The average menstrual cycle is 28 days long; however, cycles can range in length from 21 to 35 days in adults. The first day of your period is counted as day 1 of your cycle.

A cycle is divided into four phases:
- Menstrual Phase (Days 1–5): The uterine lining sheds as a period. This phase involves the release of oestrogen and progesterone.
- Follicular Phase (Days 1–13): Follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH) prompts follicles in the ovaries to mature.
- Ovulation Phase (Day 14): A surge of luteinising hormone (LH) triggers the release of an egg.
- Luteal Phase (Days 15–28): Progesterone rises to prepare the uterus for implantation. If no fertilisation occurs, progesterone and oestrogen drop, triggering menstruation.

Irregular cycles are those shorter than 21 days or longer than 35 days. Tracking cycle length, flow, and symptoms helps identify patterns that can be discussed with a healthcare provider.

SECTION 2: MENSTRUAL PAIN (DYSMENORRHOEA)
-------------------------------------------
Dysmenorrhoea refers to painful periods. Primary dysmenorrhoea involves cramping pain with no underlying condition; secondary dysmenorrhoea is caused by an underlying reproductive disorder.

Common symptoms: cramping in the lower abdomen, back pain, thigh pain, nausea, headache, fatigue.

Pain management strategies (educational purposes only):
- Heat therapy: applying a heating pad or hot water bottle to the lower abdomen has been shown to reduce prostaglandin-driven cramps.
- Gentle exercise: low-impact activities like walking or yoga can increase blood flow and reduce cramping intensity.
- Hydration: drinking sufficient water helps reduce bloating and cramping.
- Omega-3 fatty acids: found in fish, flaxseed, and walnuts; may help reduce prostaglandin production.
- Magnesium-rich foods: leafy greens, nuts, seeds — may help relax uterine muscles.

If pain is severe enough to disrupt daily activities consistently, a healthcare provider should be consulted. This is educational information only — not medical advice.

SECTION 3: HEAVY MENSTRUAL BLEEDING (MENORRHAGIA)
---------------------------------------------------
Menorrhagia is defined as menstrual bleeding that lasts more than 7 days or that is heavy enough to soak through one or more pads or tampons every hour for several consecutive hours.

Signs of heavy menstrual bleeding:
- Soaking through one or more sanitary products every hour for several hours.
- Needing to use double sanitary protection.
- Waking at night to change sanitary protection.
- Passing blood clots larger than a 50-paise coin or a quarter.
- Bleeding for more than 7 days.

Heavy periods may indicate underlying conditions such as fibroids, polyps, thyroid dysfunction, or clotting disorders. Iron deficiency anaemia is a common complication. Fatigue, weakness, pale skin, and shortness of breath may suggest low iron levels linked to heavy periods. Blood tests (ferritin, haemoglobin) can confirm iron status. A healthcare professional should be consulted if heavy bleeding is suspected.

SECTION 4: POLYCYSTIC OVARY SYNDROME (PCOS)
---------------------------------------------
PCOS is a hormonal condition affecting approximately 1 in 10 women of reproductive age. It is characterised by elevated androgen levels, infrequent or prolonged menstrual periods, and polycystic ovaries on ultrasound.

Common signs associated with PCOS:
- Irregular or missed periods (oligomenorrhoea or amenorrhoea).
- Excess facial or body hair (hirsutism) due to elevated androgens.
- Acne and oily skin.
- Thinning hair on the scalp.
- Weight gain or difficulty losing weight.
- Difficulty conceiving.

Lifestyle approaches commonly discussed for PCOS self-management:
- A balanced diet rich in fibre, lean proteins, and low-glycaemic carbohydrates may help manage insulin resistance, which is common in PCOS.
- Regular physical activity (at least 150 minutes per week of moderate exercise) can improve insulin sensitivity.
- Stress management and adequate sleep support hormonal balance.
- Tracking cycle irregularities helps clinicians assess the pattern.

PCOS cannot be confirmed without a clinical evaluation and blood tests. Please consult a healthcare provider if you suspect PCOS.

SECTION 5: PREMENSTRUAL SYNDROME (PMS) AND PMDD
-------------------------------------------------
Premenstrual syndrome (PMS) refers to a group of physical and emotional symptoms that occur in the one to two weeks before a period. Symptoms typically improve within a few days after a period begins.

Common PMS symptoms:
- Mood swings, irritability, or anxiety.
- Bloating, breast tenderness, and headaches.
- Fatigue and changes in sleep patterns.
- Food cravings.

Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder (PMDD) is a severe form of PMS in which symptoms significantly interfere with daily activities, relationships, and quality of life. PMDD involves pronounced mood-related symptoms such as depressive mood, hopelessness, anger, or anxiety. If symptoms are this severe, consulting a healthcare provider is recommended.

Lifestyle strategies for PMS:
- Regular aerobic exercise has been shown in studies to reduce PMS symptom severity.
- Calcium-rich foods (dairy, fortified plant milks, leafy greens) may reduce PMS symptoms.
- Reducing caffeine and alcohol intake during the luteal phase may help.
- Stress-reduction techniques such as mindfulness and adequate sleep support emotional balance.

SECTION 6: IRON DEFICIENCY AND MENSTRUAL HEALTH
-------------------------------------------------
Iron deficiency is the most common nutritional disorder worldwide, and menstrual blood loss is a leading cause in women of reproductive age. Heavy periods significantly increase the risk of iron deficiency anaemia.

Symptoms of iron deficiency include: fatigue, weakness, pale skin, shortness of breath, cold hands and feet, difficulty concentrating, and headaches.

Dietary sources of iron:
- Haem iron (more easily absorbed): red meat, poultry, fish, organ meats.
- Non-haem iron (plant-based): leafy greens (spinach, kale), legumes (lentils, chickpeas), tofu, fortified cereals, pumpkin seeds.

Iron absorption tips:
- Consume vitamin C (citrus, bell peppers, tomatoes) alongside non-haem iron sources to enhance absorption.
- Avoid tea, coffee, and calcium-rich foods immediately around iron-rich meals, as they can inhibit absorption.
- Phytates in whole grains (including rice, wheat) can reduce iron absorption; soaking or sprouting legumes and pairing with vitamin C helps.

Important: Iron supplementation should only be taken on the advice of a healthcare provider after blood test confirmation. Excess iron can be harmful.

SECTION 7: SLEEP AND MENSTRUAL HEALTH
---------------------------------------
Sleep quality and duration are closely linked to menstrual health. Hormone fluctuations throughout the cycle can affect sleep patterns. Many women report disturbed sleep in the week before their period (luteal phase) due to rising and falling progesterone and oestrogen levels.

Tips to improve sleep around the luteal phase:
- Maintain a consistent sleep and wake time.
- Keep the bedroom cool and dark.
- Avoid screens for 30–60 minutes before bed.
- Limit caffeine after midday.
- Gentle stretching or relaxation exercises before sleep can reduce cramping discomfort.

Chronic sleep deprivation can negatively affect hormonal balance, worsen PMS symptoms, and disrupt cycle regularity. If sleep disruption is severe and persistent, discuss with a healthcare provider.

SECTION 8: ENDOMETRIOSIS OVERVIEW
-----------------------------------
Endometriosis is a condition in which tissue similar to the uterine lining grows outside the uterus. It affects approximately 1 in 10 women of reproductive age globally and is a leading cause of chronic pelvic pain and fertility concerns.

Common symptoms associated with endometriosis:
- Painful periods (dysmenorrhoea) that significantly affect daily life.
- Chronic pelvic pain that may occur outside of periods.
- Pain during or after sexual intercourse.
- Pain during bowel movements or urination, particularly during periods.
- Heavy menstrual bleeding.
- Fatigue.
- Difficulty conceiving.

Because symptoms overlap with other conditions, endometriosis often takes years to be formally assessed by a specialist. A healthcare provider can refer to a gynaecologist for evaluation.

SECTION 9: CYCLE TRACKING AND SYMPTOM JOURNALING
--------------------------------------------------
Tracking menstrual cycles and associated symptoms is a valuable practice for understanding personal health patterns and preparing for healthcare consultations.

What to track:
- Cycle start and end dates.
- Flow intensity (none, light, medium, heavy).
- Mood, energy levels, and sleep quality.
- Pain type and intensity (scale 1–10).
- Associated symptoms (bloating, headache, nausea, etc.).
- Lifestyle factors (diet, exercise, stress).

Benefits of consistent tracking:
- Helps identify patterns in cycle length and symptom timing.
- Provides useful data to share with healthcare providers.
- Can help women anticipate and prepare for cyclical symptoms.
- May help identify conditions such as PCOS (irregular cycles), PMDD (severe luteal-phase mood changes), or endometriosis (severe pain patterns).

Tracking for 3–6 months provides a meaningful dataset for clinical review. If you notice significant changes from your normal pattern, consult a healthcare provider.

Please consult a healthcare provider for personalised medical advice.
