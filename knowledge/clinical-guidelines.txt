SECTION 1: MENORRHAGIA — ACOG & WHO CLINICAL STANDARDS
--------------------------------------------------------
Source: American College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists (ACOG) Practice Bulletin No. 128; WHO Guidelines for the Management of Iron Deficiency Anaemia (2023)

Definition (ACOG): Heavy menstrual bleeding (HMB) is defined as menstrual blood loss of 80 mL or more per cycle, or bleeding that lasts more than 7 days, or bleeding that is perceived to interfere with the woman's quality of life.

Clinical thresholds for HMB pattern flagging:
- ≥5 heavy-flow days in consecutive cycles warrants clinical evaluation.
- Pictorial Blood Assessment Chart (PBAC) score >100 correlates with objectively heavy blood loss.
- Haemoglobin <11 g/dL with menorrhagia pattern suggests iron deficiency anaemia (IDA).

Ferritin interpretation (WHO, 2023):
- <15 µg/L: Iron deficiency
- <30 µg/L: Probable iron deficiency in symptomatic women
- 30–100 µg/L: Normal range
- >100 µg/L: Elevated (possible inflammation or haemochromatosis)

Clinical indicators of menorrhagia risk pattern:
- ≥5 consecutive heavy-flow days per cycle
- Fatigue, weakness, pallor, dyspnoea on exertion co-occurring with heavy periods
- Ferritin below 30 µg/L in context of heavy periods
- Documented history of passing large clots or changing protection hourly

ACOG guidance: All women with HMB should be evaluated for structural causes (fibroids, polyps — via pelvic ultrasound) and haematological causes (coagulation disorders, thyroid dysfunction). A full blood count and serum ferritin are first-line investigations.

SECTION 2: DYSMENORRHOEA — CLINICAL CLASSIFICATION
-----------------------------------------------------
Source: ACOG Practice Bulletin; British Journal of Obstetrics & Gynaecology (BJOG) 2022 dysmenorrhoea review

Primary Dysmenorrhoea: Painful menstruation in the absence of pelvic pathology. Caused by elevated prostaglandin F2α from the endometrium during menstruation, leading to uterine contractions and ischaemia.
- Typically begins within 1–2 years of menarche.
- Pain onset: 1–2 days before or at start of menstruation; usually resolves within 72 hours.
- Associated with nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, headache.

Secondary Dysmenorrhoea: Painful menstruation due to identifiable pelvic pathology (endometriosis, adenomyosis, fibroids, pelvic inflammatory disease).
- Often begins after age 25 or with increasingly worsening pain.
- May be associated with dyspareunia, chronic pelvic pain, or infertility.

VAS Pain Score interpretation for clinical records:
- 0–3: Mild — minimal impact on function
- 4–6: Moderate — some interference with daily activities
- 7–10: Severe — significant functional impairment; warrants urgent evaluation

Clinical decision-support threshold: Reported pain consistently ≥7 on non-menstrual days over ≥2 months is considered an urgent flag for gynaecological evaluation (possible endometriosis or adenomyosis).

SECTION 3: ENDOMETRIOSIS — CLINICAL INDICATORS
------------------------------------------------
Source: ACOG Committee Opinion No. 760; European Society of Human Reproduction and Embryology (ESHRE) Endometriosis Guideline 2022

Endometriosis affects an estimated 10% of women of reproductive age globally (WHO, 2023). Average delay from symptom onset to diagnosis is 6–10 years.

Key clinical indicators (decision-support language):
- Chronic pelvic pain (≥6 months) not adequately explained by other pathology.
- Severe dysmenorrhoea (VAS ≥7) that does not respond to standard analgesia.
- Dyspareunia (painful intercourse) — deep dyspareunia especially.
- Dyschezia: painful defecation during menstruation.
- Subfertility or infertility with previous ectopic or pelvic adhesions.
- Non-period pain days ≥5/month with pain ≥6/10 consistently.

ESHRE 2022 guidance: Symptoms alone cannot definitively confirm endometriosis; however, women presenting with ≥3 of the above indicators should be referred for specialist gynaecological evaluation, which may include laparoscopy.

SECTION 4: PMDD — DSM-5 AND RCOG CRITERIA
-------------------------------------------
Source: DSM-5 (APA, 2013); Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists (RCOG) GTG No. 48 — Management of PMS, 2016

Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder (PMDD) requires:
- ≥5 symptoms in the final week before menses onset.
- Improvement within a few days after onset.
- Minimal or absent in the week post-menses.
- ≥1 of: marked affective lability, marked irritability/anger, marked depressed mood or hopelessness, marked anxiety or tension.
- Symptoms cause marked interference with work, school, social activities, or relationships.
- Not attributable to another mental health disorder.

Clinical decision-support indicators for PMDD pattern:
- Luteal mood score significantly lower than follicular mood score over ≥2 cycles.
- Luteal-phase mood score ≤2.0 (out of 5) consistently.
- Follicular/luteal mood differential ≥1.5 points on standardised scale.
- Patient-reported significant interference with work or relationships.

RCOG guidance: PMDD is managed with SSRIs (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors) taken continuously or in the luteal phase, lifestyle modifications (exercise, reduced caffeine/alcohol), and CBT. Referral to a women's mental health service may be appropriate in severe cases.

SECTION 5: IRON DEFICIENCY ANAEMIA — NIH & WHO STANDARDS
----------------------------------------------------------
Source: NIH Office of Dietary Supplements — Iron Fact Sheet for Health Professionals; WHO Guideline for Iron Supplementation (2023)

Iron Deficiency Anaemia (IDA) clinical thresholds (WHO, 2023):
- Haemoglobin <12 g/dL in non-pregnant women of reproductive age = anaemia.
- Haemoglobin <8 g/dL = severe anaemia requiring urgent evaluation.
- Ferritin <15 µg/L = iron deficiency (absolute).
- Ferritin <30 µg/L = likely iron deficiency with concomitant chronic disease or inflammation.

Daily iron requirements (NIH):
- Women aged 19–50: 18 mg/day elemental iron (higher for vegetarians due to lower haem iron absorption).
- Pregnant women: 27 mg/day.
- Post-menopausal women: 8 mg/day.

Foods high in haem iron (absorption ~25%): beef, organ meats (liver, kidney), dark poultry, fish, shellfish.

Foods high in non-haem iron (absorption ~2–20%): fortified cereals, legumes (lentils, chickpeas, kidney beans), tofu, spinach, pumpkin seeds.

Absorption enhancers: Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) consumed simultaneously significantly increases non-haem iron absorption.

Absorption inhibitors: Phytates (found in whole grains, legumes), polyphenols (in tea, coffee, wine), calcium-rich foods — all reduce non-haem iron absorption when consumed at the same time as iron-rich foods.

Clinical pattern indicators suggesting IDA risk in digital health records:
- ≥5 heavy-flow days with concurrent low energy days ≥3/week.
- Patient-reported fatigue, pallor, or shortness of breath during or after period.
- Staple grain diet (rice, wheat) without concurrent vitamin C intake.
- Vegetarian or vegan diet without iron supplementation tracking.

SECTION 6: PCOS — ROTTERDAM CRITERIA & AE-PCOS STANDARDS
----------------------------------------------------------
Source: Rotterdam ESHRE/ASRM-Sponsored PCOS Consensus Workshop Group, 2004; Androgen Excess PCOS Society (AE-PCOS) updated 2022 guidelines; ACOG Practice Bulletin No. 194

PCOS diagnosis requires ≥2 of 3 Rotterdam criteria (requires clinical evaluation):
1. Oligo- or anovulation (irregular/absent periods).
2. Clinical or biochemical signs of hyperandrogenism (hirsutism, acne, elevated testosterone).
3. Polycystic ovaries on ultrasound (≥12 follicles on one ovary, or ovarian volume >10 mL).

AE-PCOS updated position: Hyperandrogenism is considered central to "true" PCOS. Normal androgen levels with polycystic morphology and oligomenorrhoea may represent a distinct phenotype.

Metabolic comorbidities to screen for in PCOS (ACOG, 2018):
- Insulin resistance and type 2 diabetes: Screen with fasting glucose and 2-hour OGTT.
- Dyslipidaemia: Fasting lipid panel.
- Obstructive sleep apnea: high prevalence in PCOS even in normal-weight women.
- Endometrial hyperplasia risk: women with prolonged anovulation may need endometrial assessment.
- Psychological health: Depression and anxiety are significantly more prevalent in PCOS; psychological screening recommended.

Decision-support indicators for PCOS pattern from symptom logs:
- Cycles consistently longer than 35 days or absent (oligomenorrhoea/amenorrhoea).
- Patient-reported symptoms: excessive hair growth, acne, weight changes.
- Low energy and mood patterns consistent with insulin resistance.
- Irregular flow patterns.

SECTION 7: CYCLE TRACKING CLINICAL UTILITY
-------------------------------------------
Source: ACOG Committee Opinion No. 651; WHO recommendations on menstrual health

Clinical utility of tracking data:
- Cycle length variability >7 days is a clinical definition of irregular cycles.
- Prospective symptom tracking over ≥2 cycles is required for PMDD diagnosis (RCOG).
- Pain diary data (daily VAS scores) is recommended by ESHRE for assessing endometriosis pre-operatively.
- Basal body temperature and LH surge detection can assist in confirming ovulation.

Data elements recommended for clinician handover:
1. Cycle length (days): minimum 3 cycles.
2. Bleed duration and heaviness score.
3. Daily pain score (0–10 VAS) per cycle phase.
4. Mood and energy ratings per day.
5. Symptom occurrence (nausea, headache, bloating, fatigue, etc.).
6. Notes on lifestyle factors: sleep, diet, exercise, stress events.

SECTION 8: URGENT CLINICAL FLAGS
----------------------------------
Source: ACOG, ESHRE, RCOG guidelines

The following patterns in symptom tracking data suggest urgent clinical referral:
- Pain VAS ≥8 on non-period days occurring for ≥2 consecutive months.
- Haemoglobin <8 g/dL or ferritin <15 µg/L confirmed on blood testing.
- Sudden change in cycle length (>10 days shorter or longer than usual).
- Intermenstrual bleeding (bleeding between periods) occurring consistently.
- Postcoital bleeding.
- Pelvic pain radiating to the back or thigh outside of menstruation.
- Signs of shock associated with heavy bleeding (dizziness, fainting, pallor).
- Amenorrhoea >90 days in a previously cycling woman (rule out pregnancy, thyroid dysfunction, hyperprolactinaemia, hypothalamic amenorrhoea).

Note: These flags are for clinical decision-support purposes. Individual assessment by a qualified healthcare professional is required for any clinical determination.
